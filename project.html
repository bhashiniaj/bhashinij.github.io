Project Overview

About the Project

This Final Year Project addresses the design and prototype implementation of a photovoltaic (PV) integrated electric vehicle (EV) charging system, with specific application to light EVs — including e-bicycles and electric three-wheelers — in the Sri Lankan energy context.

The system integrates a 300 W flexible mono-crystalline silicon PV array, a custom-designed non-isolated DC–DC boost converter employing Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT), an EDLC supercapacitor transient buffer, and a 48 V LiFePO₄ battery pack. The Perturb & Observe (P&O) algorithm is implemented for MPPT, with the converter designed to step up the PV output voltage (18–36 V) to a stable 48 V DC bus.

The incorporation of a supercapacitor buffer serves to absorb transient current demands, thereby reducing peak stress on the battery cells and contributing to an estimated 20–30% improvement in battery cycle life. The full system is designed and validated through simulation in MATLAB/Simulink prior to hardware realisation.

Design Objectives

Project Objectives

Solar Energy Harvesting

Design a 1.5 m² flexible mono-crystalline silicon PV array (300 W, 20% efficiency) with MPPT to harvest approximately 1.5 kWh per day under Sri Lanka's five peak sun hours, providing an estimated 18.75 km of additional daily EV range.

DC–DC Power Conversion

Design and implement a non-isolated boost converter (L = 45 µH, C = 163 µF, fsw = 20 kHz) capable of stepping up the PV terminal voltage (18–36 V) to a regulated 48 V DC bus with an estimated efficiency of 90–95%.

Battery Management & Buffering

Integrate a 48 V LiFePO₄ battery pack (15S configuration, 6 Ah, 288 Wh) with an EDLC supercapacitor transient buffer to absorb peak current demands, reduce battery stress, and extend operational cycle life by an estimated 20–30%.
